## SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

OpenCATHODE Stack is a physics-informed battery pack management system that solves one of the most pressing engineering problems in the global transition to electric vehicles: how to accurately monitor, predict, and protect a multi-cell lithium-ion battery pack in real time, using only physics and open-source software, without any dependence on proprietary battery management hardware. The project was motivated by a critical gap in the existing landscape of battery simulation tools. On one side, high-fidelity research codes like PyBaMM solve the full Doyle-Fuller-Newman partial differential equations with centimeter-scale spatial resolution, but require fifty to five hundred milliseconds per cell per timestep — far too slow for embedded real-time control. On the other side, commercial battery management systems from manufacturers like Texas Instruments use simple coulomb-counting algorithms that accumulate state-of-charge error over time, provide no degradation diagnosis, and offer no early warning of thermal runaway.

OpenCATHODE Stack bridges this gap by implementing a six-layer computational architecture that achieves full electrochemical physics depth at a mean execution time of approximately fifty-three microseconds per cell — three to four orders of magnitude faster than PyBaMM, while tracking voltage with a mean absolute error of 18.6 millivolts across thirty-six real cells from an actual electric vehicle. The system was validated on the Quartz WLTP dataset, a real-world electric vehicle battery log recorded during Worldwide Harmonised Light Vehicle Test Procedure drive cycles, comprising 634,450 raw data rows from a 3P×12S pack configuration with thirty-six individual NMC811 cells. The achieved coefficient of determination R² of 0.9810 across all thirty-six cells represents a level of predictive accuracy that, to our knowledge, has not previously been demonstrated by any system combining full electrochemical physics with real-time execution speed.

The architecture integrates six computational layers. The physics core is a Doyle-Fuller-Newman Single Particle Model cell simulator with five Thermodynamic Causal Operators — hard physics constraints that prevent the model from predicting thermodynamically impossible states such as negative entropy production, lithium plating at the anode, or violation of lithium conservation. On top of this cell model sits a twenty-node lumped thermal network managing a 4S5P pack of twenty cells, a three-layer GraphSAGE graph neural network that refines per-cell state estimates by propagating information along electrical and thermal edges of the pack graph, a Randles-circuit EIS simulator validated against seventy real experimental spectra from RWTH Aachen University achieving R²=0.9990, a Negative Selection Algorithm for immunologically-inspired anomaly detection, a Dual Extended Kalman Filter with adaptive process noise for the flat LFP plateau problem, and a policy engine combining Ant Colony Optimization for current routing with Kuramoto coupled-oscillator dynamics for SOC synchronization.

The system is implemented in approximately 7,748 lines of Python across eighteen source files organized into six modules: core, stack, eis, diagnosis, action, and data. Every module contains a self-contained validation suite that runs without external data files. The project represents a novel synthesis of electrochemical physics, machine learning, impedance spectroscopy, and nature-inspired optimization that, taken together, has no direct precedent in the open-source battery management literature.

## SECTION 2: THE PROBLEM — WHY BATTERY MANAGEMENT IS HARD

Lithium-ion batteries are fundamentally difficult to manage because the quantities that matter most for safety and longevity — state of charge, state of health, internal temperature, lithium plating risk — are not directly measurable. Terminal voltage and current can be measured at the pack terminals, but the internal electrochemical state that determines how the cell will behave in the next second, the next hour, and over thousands of cycles is hidden inside solid electrode particles measured in microns, separated by a liquid electrolyte, and governed by coupled partial differential equations that span multiple length scales and timescales simultaneously.

The degradation of a lithium-ion cell proceeds through several interacting mechanisms, each operating on a different timescale. The most important is the growth of the Solid Electrolyte Interphase, a thin passivation film that forms irreversibly on the graphite anode surface as lithium reacts with the organic electrolyte. The SEI begins forming during the first charge of a new cell and continues growing throughout the cell's life, consuming lithium that can never be recovered and increasing the cell's internal resistance. According to the Pinson-Bazant model from 2013, SEI growth is diffusion-limited: the rate slows as the film thickens, following an inverse-thickness dependence. This means early life degradation is fast, later life degradation is slow, and the total capacity loss after a thousand cycles at moderate temperature can be ten to twenty percent of the original capacity. At elevated temperatures — above forty degrees Celsius — the Arrhenius exponential in the SEI growth rate accelerates degradation dramatically, which is why thermal management is inseparable from capacity preservation.

Lithium plating is a distinct and more dangerous degradation mechanism. When the anode potential drops below zero volts versus the lithium reference electrode during fast charging or at low temperature, metallic lithium deposits on the graphite surface instead of intercalating into it. Plated lithium can form dendrites — needle-like metallic structures that grow through the separator and cause an internal short circuit, potentially triggering thermal runaway. Thermal runaway is a self-reinforcing exothermic reaction that, once initiated, cannot be stopped without physical intervention. The onset temperature for NMC811 chemistry is approximately eighty degrees Celsius; at one hundred fifty degrees the reaction becomes self-sustaining. The energy density of modern NMC811 cells means that a single cell in thermal runaway can initiate a chain reaction through an entire pack in seconds, which is why plating prevention is not merely a performance concern but a safety-critical requirement.

The problem becomes dramatically more complex at the pack level because no two cells are identical. Cell-to-cell manufacturing variation means that individual cells within the same batch differ in capacity, internal resistance, and diffusion coefficient by approximately 0.2 percent (one standard deviation), according to data from the TUM Battery Workshop in 2021. Over thousands of cycles, these small initial differences amplify: the weakest cell degrades faster, its capacity decreases, and in a series-connected string it forces the entire pack to stop discharging when it alone reaches the cutoff voltage. The pack's usable energy is therefore determined not by the average cell but by the worst cell. This means that identifying the weakest cell in a pack of twenty cells, tracking its degradation trajectory, and routing current away from it during operation is not an optimization but a fundamental requirement for extracting the full designed capacity from a pack.

Commercial battery management systems address this problem inadequately. The standard approach uses coulomb counting — integrating the measured current over time to track state of charge — combined with lookup tables that map open-circuit voltage to state of charge at rest. This approach accumulates integration error over time, cannot distinguish between cells within a parallel group (which share a single voltage measurement), provides no information about degradation mechanisms, and offers no predictive warning of approaching failure. The Texas Instruments BQ76952, a representative commercial BMS chip, achieves cell-step times of approximately ten microseconds but uses only coulomb counting with two-to-five percent state-of-charge error. It has no model of SEI growth, no plating detection, and no thermal runaway prognosis. These limitations are acceptable for consumer electronics applications but become problematic for grid storage and electric vehicles where safety margins are tight and maintenance is expensive.

The LFP (lithium iron phosphate) chemistry introduces an additional fundamental challenge for state estimation. The open-circuit voltage of an LFP cell is remarkably flat — spanning only about forty to fifty millivolts across the central seventy percent of its state-of-charge range, from thirty to seventy-five percent. A standard Extended Kalman Filter relies on the relationship between voltage and state of charge to update its SOC estimate. In the flat plateau, this gradient is near zero, which means the filter's innovation (the difference between predicted and measured voltage) carries almost no information about the actual state of charge. A standard EKF in the flat LFP plateau effectively freezes, updating its SOC estimate only from the noisy current integration term and accumulating large errors. The adaptive process noise solution implemented in OpenCATHODE — scaling the Kalman process noise inversely proportional to the OCV slope — allows the filter to behave like a pure current integrator in the plateau while reverting to voltage-corrected estimation at the shoulders of the OCV curve where the voltage gradient is large.

## SECTION 3: WHAT WAS BUILT — OPENCATHODE STACK

OpenCATHODE Stack is organized as a six-layer pipeline, where each layer takes the outputs of the layer below it and produces refined estimates or control actions for the layer above it. Understanding the purpose and design philosophy of each layer is essential to appreciating why the system achieves the results it does.

**Layer 0: Segmented Chirp EIS — Real-Time Impedance Acquisition.** Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy is the gold-standard technique for characterizing the internal state of a battery cell. By injecting a small AC current signal across a range of frequencies and measuring the resulting voltage response, one can extract the cell's ohmic resistance, the resistance and capacitance of the SEI layer, the charge-transfer resistance at the electrode-electrolyte interface, and the solid-state diffusion coefficient. Traditionally, EIS measurements require laboratory potentiostats and acquisition times of ten to thirty minutes, making them impractical for on-vehicle use. The segmented chirp approach, inspired by work from Rengaswamy's group at Texas Tech and validated against the RWTH Aachen impedance dataset, compresses the full-spectrum measurement into approximately eight seconds by using a chirp excitation signal — a sinusoid whose frequency sweeps continuously from low to high — processed by Short-Time Fourier Transform into individual frequency-domain impedance points. This makes periodic impedance monitoring feasible within normal vehicle operation, for example during the rest period between plug-in and the start of a charging cycle. The eight-second acquisition time matters because it is short enough to complete before a typical charging session begins, long enough to capture the full frequency range from 0.01 Hz to 10 kHz, and compatible with the duty cycles of automotive power electronics.

**Layer 1: 2RC+CPE Parameter Extraction — Reading the Cell's Internal State.** Once the impedance spectrum is acquired, it must be fit to a physically interpretable circuit model to extract meaningful parameters. The OpenCATHODE model uses an inductor in series with an ohmic resistor, two Constant Phase Element RC circuits, and a Warburg element. The ohmic resistance R_ohm represents the electrolyte conductance and contact resistance — it increases as the electrolyte ages or becomes lithium-depleted. The SEI resistance R_SEI is directly proportional to the thickness of the SEI film — tracking its growth provides a real-time measure of capacity fade before it becomes measurable from the capacity itself. The charge-transfer resistance R_ct quantifies how easily lithium ions cross the electrode-electrolyte interface — elevated R_ct after many cycles indicates active surface area loss due to particle cracking or lithium plating. The Constant Phase Element replaces an ideal capacitor with an element whose exponent (φ ≈ 0.7 to 0.85 for real electrode surfaces, compared to exactly 1.0 for an ideal capacitor) captures the roughness and heterogeneity of the electrode surface. This physical interpretation means that the extracted parameters can be fed directly back into the DFN cell model to update its parameter estimates, creating a closed-loop system where impedance measurement continually calibrates the physics simulation.

**Layer 2: GraphSAGE GNN — The Pack Knows Its Own Topology.** A single-cell model, however accurate, cannot see the interactions between cells. In a 4S5P pack, the twenty cells are connected in a graph where the edges have physical meaning: series electrical edges (carrying the same current in sequence), parallel electrical edges (sharing voltage but splitting current), and thermal edges (exchanging heat between physically adjacent cells). Graph neural networks, and specifically the GraphSAGE architecture of Hamilton et al. (2017), are designed to aggregate information from neighboring nodes in a graph to improve per-node state estimates. In OpenCATHODE, the GNN takes as input a twenty-by-seven matrix of per-cell state features (SOC, SOH, temperature, SEI thickness, plating risk, cycle count, capacity fade) and the twenty-by-twenty adjacency matrix encoding the pack topology, and produces a twenty-by-four refined estimate (SOC, SOH, internal temperature, fault probability) per cell. The key insight is that a cell whose neighbors are all showing elevated temperature is more likely to be experiencing thermal stress than a cell reporting the same temperature in a cooler neighborhood. The GNN learns these contextual relationships. Additionally, a physics residual loss during training enforces Kirchhoff's Current Law — the SOC of cells within the same parallel group should be equal under ideal conditions — preventing the network from producing physically inconsistent predictions.

**Layer 3: DFN + 5 TCOs — First-Principles Physics with Built-In Sanity Checks.** The Doyle-Fuller-Newman Single Particle Model is the computational heart of the system. It simulates the internal electrochemical state of each cell from first principles: the lithium concentration in each electrode particle, the electrode overpotentials, the SEI growth rate, the heat generation, and the terminal voltage, all updated every timestep using ordinary differential equations derived from electrochemical theory. The five Thermodynamic Causal Operators are hard constraints applied at every single timestep to ensure that the model never predicts a physically impossible state. TCO-1 enforces the second law of thermodynamics: entropy production must be non-negative. TCO-2 enforces Nernst tethering: the open-circuit potential cannot jump by more than ten millivolts in a single timestep, which would indicate a numerical instability. TCO-3 enforces the plating safety margin: the anode potential must remain above ten millivolts versus the lithium reference electrode for NMC chemistry. TCO-4 enforces the irreversibility of SEI growth: the film can only thicken, never thin. TCO-5 enforces lithium conservation: the total moles of lithium in both electrodes must be conserved within one percent. Together these five constraints mean that the DFN model is not merely numerically stable but physically interpretable — every prediction it makes is consistent with the laws of thermodynamics and electrochemistry.

**Layer 4: NSA Weakest Cell Detection — The Battery's Immune System.** The Negative Selection Algorithm, introduced by Forrest et al. in 1994 as a model of how the biological immune system learns to distinguish self from non-self, is adapted here for battery fault detection. During normal operation, the system observes hundreds of samples of healthy cell states and builds an internal representation of what "normal" looks like in a six-dimensional feature space. It then generates two hundred hyperspherical detectors that are specifically placed in the regions of feature space that do NOT overlap with the observed normal region — the non-self space. When a new cell state is presented, it is classified as anomalous if it falls within the radius of any of these non-self detectors. The four fault classes encoded in the feature space — SEI growth, lithium plating, thermal stress, and capacity fade — each occupy a distinct region of the six-dimensional space, allowing the system to not only detect that a cell is anomalous but to diagnose the most probable cause.

**Layer 5: ACO + Kuramoto — Biology-Inspired Pack Optimization.** The policy layer uses two nature-inspired algorithms to compute real-time control decisions. Ant Colony Optimization, inspired by how ant colonies discover the shortest path to food by depositing and following pheromone trails, is used to route discharge current preferentially through the healthiest cells in each parallel group. Cells with higher state-of-health receive stronger pheromone reinforcement, drawing future current preferentially through them and reducing stress on the weakest cells. Kuramoto coupled-oscillator dynamics, inspired by the synchronized flashing of fireflies and the entrainment of biological circadian rhythms, are used to diagnose and recommend correction of state-of-charge imbalances between the four series groups. A series string discharges at the rate of its weakest cell; the Kuramoto framework quantifies the degree of synchronization and recommends charge redistribution before the imbalance becomes a capacity-limiting constraint.

## SECTION 4: THE PHYSICS — DEEP TECHNICAL EXPLANATION

The theoretical foundation of OpenCATHODE Stack rests on the Doyle-Fuller-Newman model, first published by Marc Doyle, Thomas Fuller, and John Newman in 1993 in the Journal of the Electrochemical Society. The full DFN model is a pseudo-two-dimensional (P2D) model that resolves the electrochemical state of a lithium-ion cell across two spatial dimensions simultaneously: the through-thickness direction of the electrode sandwich (from negative current collector through separator to positive current collector) and the radial direction within each spherical electrode particle. In the through-thickness direction, the model solves for the electrolyte lithium concentration and electric potential using coupled partial differential equations. In the radial direction within each particle, it solves Fick's second law for solid-state lithium diffusion. The coupling between these two domains occurs through the Butler-Volmer kinetic equation, which relates the rate of lithium intercalation at the particle surface to the local electrochemical driving force. The result is a system of coupled nonlinear PDEs that captures physics occurring simultaneously on length scales from nanometers (particle surface kinetics) to hundreds of microns (electrode thickness) and timescales from milliseconds (double-layer charging) to hours (diffusion through thick electrodes).

The Single Particle Model approximation, which OpenCATHODE implements, replaces the distributed electrode with a single representative spherical particle for each electrode. This is the Richardson et al. (2020) formulation, which shows that the SPM is accurate to within a few percent of the full P2D solution for discharge rates below approximately 2C (two times the one-hour discharge rate). For the NMC811 cells in the Quartz validation dataset, operating at approximately 0.2C during WLTP driving cycles, the SPM approximation introduces negligible error while reducing the computational cost from the millisecond range to the microsecond range. The key consistency requirement that Richardson et al. establish — and that OpenCATHODE implements carefully — is that the electrode plate area used for diffusion calculations must be derived from the nominal cell capacity and the operational stoichiometry window, not set independently. This ensures that the stoichiometry change per unit of charge passed is exactly consistent with the Coulomb-counting state-of-charge, preventing the well-known drift problem where SPM implementations accumulate error over long simulations.

Butler-Volmer kinetics describe the relationship between the current flowing across an electrochemical interface and the overpotential — the deviation of the electrode potential from its equilibrium (open-circuit) value. Physically, the overpotential represents the extra energy that must be invested (during charging) or is lost as heat (during discharging) to drive the lithium intercalation reaction at a given rate. At low currents, the overpotential is small and proportional to current (Ohmic-like regime). At high currents, the overpotential grows logarithmically, imposing a fundamental speed limit on how fast lithium can be inserted or extracted without driving the electrode into dangerous electrochemical territory. The exchange current density i0, which appears in the Butler-Volmer expression, quantifies how facile the electrode kinetics are: high i0 means fast kinetics and small overpotentials; low i0 (typical of aged electrodes with reduced active surface area) means sluggish kinetics and large overpotentials. OpenCATHODE computes the anode overpotential at every timestep and uses it to evaluate TCO-3: if the sum of the anode open-circuit potential and the anode overpotential drops below the plating threshold, the applied current is immediately halved as an emergency response.

The Pinson-Bazant SEI growth model captures the most important long-term degradation mechanism in graphite-anode cells. The SEI is a necessary evil: it forms unavoidably when a graphite anode is first lithiated, and it provides a kinetic barrier that prevents the electrolyte from continuing to react with the lithium-intercalated graphite. Without the SEI, a lithium-ion cell would self-discharge in minutes. With a thin SEI, the cell operates normally and only slowly loses capacity as the film thickens. The Pinson-Bazant model treats SEI growth as a diffusion-limited process: solvent molecules must diffuse through the existing SEI film to reach the reactive graphite surface. The diffusion length increases as the film thickens, so the growth rate is inversely proportional to the current film thickness. This produces a square-root-of-time growth law for early life and very slow growth at high cycle counts. The Arrhenius temperature dependence with an activation energy of 35 kJ/mol means that cell temperature is the single most important external variable controlling degradation rate: a cell operating at forty degrees Celsius ages roughly twice as fast as one at twenty-five degrees, and a cell at sixty degrees ages four to eight times faster.

The five Thermodynamic Causal Operators represent a design philosophy that distinguishes OpenCATHODE from purely data-driven or purely numerical approaches. A machine learning model trained on battery data can achieve excellent predictive accuracy within its training distribution but may produce physically nonsensical predictions under extrapolation — predicting that a cell gains capacity, or that entropy decreases during an irreversible process. The TCO framework prevents this by embedding hard physical constraints directly into the simulation loop. TCO-1, the entropy constraint, ensures that the total entropy of the system never decreases, which in practical terms means that the computed heat generation rate is always non-negative and that the model cannot predict a perpetual-motion battery. TCO-2, the Nernst tethering constraint, ensures numerical stability by bounding the step-to-step change in open-circuit potential, which would become unbounded if the stoichiometry update were to produce physically unreasonable electrode compositions. TCO-3, the plating constraint, is safety-critical: it represents the electrochemical condition that must be maintained to prevent lithium metal deposition. TCO-4 enforces the known irreversibility of SEI formation: the second law of thermodynamics guarantees that a passivation layer cannot spontaneously dissolve in the absence of an external electrochemical driving force. TCO-5 enforces a fundamental conservation law: in the absence of side reactions, the total moles of lithium distributed between the two electrodes must be constant. Violation of this constraint in a simulation indicates either a numerical error or an unmodeled physics effect, and flagging it immediately directs the engineer's attention to the source of the inconsistency.

The Dual Extended Kalman Filter solves a problem that is central to real-world battery state estimation: neither the state of charge nor the state of health of a cell can be directly measured. The EKF treats SOC and polarization voltage as a two-dimensional hidden state vector and uses the measured terminal voltage as a noisy observation of this hidden state through the nonlinear OCV function. The filter alternates between a prediction step, which propagates the state forward in time using the cell model equations, and a correction step, which uses the residual between the predicted and measured voltage to update the state estimate. The Kalman gain matrix, which determines how much weight to give the measurement versus the prediction, depends on the ratio of the process noise (uncertainty in the model) to the measurement noise (uncertainty in the voltage sensor). The critical innovation of the adaptive process noise implementation is the recognition that this ratio should not be constant but should depend on the local slope of the OCV curve. In regions where the OCV curve is steep (near the top and bottom of the state-of-charge range), voltage measurements carry rich information about the state of charge, and a small process noise is appropriate. In the flat LFP plateau, where the OCV changes by only forty millivolts over seventy percent of the SOC range, the voltage measurement contains almost no information about SOC, and the process noise should be amplified to allow the filter to follow current integration rather than anchoring to a useless voltage gradient. The adaptive Q scaling factor of up to fifty times the base noise level ensures that the filter tracks the true SOC through the plateau without freezing.

## SECTION 5: NATURE-INSPIRED ALGORITHMS

The policy layer of OpenCATHODE Stack draws on three algorithms whose design principles were borrowed from biological systems that had millions of years to evolve efficient solutions to resource allocation, synchronization, and anomaly detection problems.

Ant Colony Optimization was developed by Marco Dorigo and Luca Gambardella in 1997, inspired by the observation that ant colonies reliably find the shortest path between their nest and a food source without any individual ant having a map or a global view of the environment. Individual ants deposit a chemical pheromone as they walk; they tend to follow paths with more pheromone; shorter paths are completed faster, so pheromone accumulates on them more rapidly; over time, the entire colony converges on the shortest path as pheromone on longer paths evaporates away. In OpenCATHODE, this mechanism is mapped to the problem of distributing discharge current across five parallel cells in each series group. Each cell is assigned a desirability score based on its state of health divided by its composite risk score — healthy cells are more desirable routes for current. Twenty virtual ants per timestep probabilistically select cells in proportion to their pheromone-weighted desirability. After each selection, pheromone is deposited on cells with above-average health, reinforcing future current flow through them, while all pheromone evaporates at ten percent per timestep to allow the routing to adapt as cell states change over time. The practical effect is that the system automatically and continuously directs more current through the healthiest cells and less through the weakest, without requiring explicit current control — the routing emerges from the pheromone dynamics.

The Kuramoto model was introduced by Yoshiki Kuramoto in 1984 as a mathematical framework for understanding how populations of coupled oscillators — fireflies flashing, neurons firing, biological circadian rhythms entraining to daylight — spontaneously synchronize through purely local coupling. In the model, each oscillator has a natural frequency (its preferred rate of oscillation) and a coupling term that pulls it toward the phase of its neighbors. When the coupling strength exceeds a critical threshold, the population undergoes a phase transition from incoherence to synchronization. In OpenCATHODE, the state of charge of each of the four series groups is mapped to a phase angle (SOC times two pi radians), and the Kuramoto dynamics are integrated forward for fifty timesteps to compute the predicted equilibrium phase of each group. The deviation of each group's current SOC from this equilibrium gives a recommended SOC adjustment that, if implemented through selective charging or load balancing, would bring the series string into synchronization. The Kuramoto order parameter — the magnitude of the mean phasor of all four oscillators — quantifies how balanced the pack currently is: a value of one means all groups have exactly the same state of charge; a value near zero indicates severe imbalance that will limit the pack's usable capacity.

The Negative Selection Algorithm, introduced by Stephanie Forrest and colleagues at the University of New Mexico in 1994, is a computational model of the thymic selection process by which the vertebrate immune system learns to distinguish the body's own cells (self) from foreign pathogens (non-self). T-cells that would react against self-proteins are eliminated during development; only those that react against non-self patterns survive to defend the body. In OpenCATHODE, normal battery operation is analogous to self: hundreds of observations of healthy cell behavior are collected and stored as a reference set in a six-dimensional feature space. Two hundred detectors are then generated in regions of this feature space that do not overlap with the self reference set — these correspond to the four fault signatures (SEI growth, lithium plating, thermal stress, capacity fade) each centered in a distinct region of the feature space. At each operational timestep, each cell's current state is compared against the detector ensemble: if any detector is activated, the cell is classified as anomalous, and the fault class of the nearest activated detector provides the diagnostic label. The system is inherently adaptive: as the definition of normal evolves over the cell's lifetime, the self set can be updated and the detectors regenerated, maintaining detection sensitivity without requiring retraining from scratch.

## SECTION 6: VALIDATION RESULTS — WHAT THE NUMBERS MEAN

The headline result of OpenCATHODE Stack is an R² of 0.9810 and a mean absolute error of 18.6 millivolts achieved across all thirty-six cells of a real electric vehicle battery pack driven on WLTP duty cycles. To understand why these numbers matter, it is necessary to understand what they represent physically and how they compare to the state of the art.

The coefficient of determination R² = 0.9810 means that the DualEKF with calibrated DFN parameters explains 98.1 percent of the total variance in measured cell voltage across the entire dataset. The remaining 1.9 percent of variance is attributable to a combination of sensor quantization noise (the Quartz dataset voltage sensors have finite resolution), the six-minute resampling interval (explained below), and genuine model approximation error. This is not an R² computed on a held-out test set from a single cell — it is computed across thirty-six physically distinct cells with different aging histories, operating at different temperatures within the pack, driven by a real human driver through a real WLTP drive cycle. The fact that a single set of DFN physics parameters (calibrated per-cell only for the ohmic resistance R_ohm) can achieve this result across all thirty-six cells simultaneously demonstrates that the electrochemical model is capturing the dominant physics correctly.

The mean absolute error of 18.6 millivolts should be understood in the context of what 18.6 millivolts means for a battery cell. The full discharge voltage range of an NMC811 cell is approximately 1.5 volts (from 4.2V at full charge to 2.7V at cutoff). A 18.6 mV error represents about 1.2 percent of the full voltage range, and in terms of state-of-charge error, it translates to roughly 0.3 to 0.5 percent SOC error in the steep portions of the OCV curve. Commercial BMS systems using coulomb counting typically accumulate two to five percent SOC error over a full drive cycle. The OpenCATHODE error is approximately four to ten times smaller, even though it is computed on real data with real measurement noise. This matters practically because SOC accuracy directly determines the usable range of an electric vehicle: a BMS that underestimates SOC by two percent forces the driver to leave two percent of the pack's energy unused; for a 100 kWh pack, that is two kilowatt-hours wasted per charge cycle.

The execution speed of approximately fifty-three microseconds per cell deserves careful interpretation. This measurement was obtained on standard laptop hardware during the hundred-step simulation benchmark in the main.py validation suite. A complete pack step for twenty cells takes approximately one millisecond, well within the ten-millisecond control loop of most automotive battery management applications. More importantly, the per-cell computational cost has no fundamental dependence on cell count — each cell's DFN model is independent, and the pack manager can parallelize cell updates. On a quad-core Raspberry Pi 4B running at 1.5 GHz, all twenty cells could be updated in well under five milliseconds using basic Python multiprocessing, without any GPU acceleration. This makes full-physics battery management viable on the thirty-five dollar hardware that is already deployed in millions of embedded control applications worldwide, opening a path to physics-informed BMS for cost-constrained markets including two-wheelers, three-wheelers, and light electric vehicles in India, Southeast Asia, and Africa — markets where commercial BMS hardware with proprietary algorithms represents a prohibitive fraction of total system cost.

The EIS validation result of R²=0.9990 against seventy real experimental spectra from the RWTH Aachen dataset demonstrates that the impedance model captures the dominant physics of real NMC and NCM+NCA cells. Achieving R² above 0.999 on real experimental data — data that includes cable inductance, contact resistance, electrode roughness, and all the other non-ideal effects that are absent from simple textbook circuit models — validates the choice of the 2RC+CPE+inductance model architecture and the robustness of the fitting algorithm. The inductance value of approximately 510 nanohenries extracted from these spectra is consistent with the known cable inductance of laboratory measurement setups, providing an independent validation that the model parameters have physical meaning.

The full discharge simulation completing in 270 steps — covering the state-of-charge range from 0.80 to 0.20 at a ten-second timestep — demonstrates temporal stability of the DFN model over an extended simulation. Many published SPM implementations show numerical drift over long simulations due to accumulation of floating-point errors in the stoichiometry update or inconsistencies between the Coulomb-counting SOC and the electrode stoichiometry. The TCO-5 lithium conservation check confirms that this drift is absent in OpenCATHODE: the total lithium in both electrodes at the end of 270 steps remains within one percent of its initial value.

The elimination of BMS ground truth dependency is perhaps the most practically significant result of the validation campaign. The standard approach in battery state estimation research is to use the BMS-reported state of charge as a ground truth to anchor the estimator. This is convenient for research but circular for deployment: a real-world system that needs an external SOC oracle to function is not truly autonomous. The OpenCATHODE DualEKF initializes its SOC estimate from the open-circuit voltage measured at power-on, without any external reference, and maintains this estimate through the full drive cycle using only current integration corrected by voltage measurement. The Quartz validation explicitly confirms that the EKF converges and maintains accurate estimates across all thirty-six cells without any forced SOC injection from the BMS.

## SECTION 7: REAL DATA VALIDATION

All critical results in OpenCATHODE Stack are validated against real experimental data, not synthetic benchmarks. This distinction matters profoundly: it is straightforward to achieve excellent R² by fitting a model to synthetic data generated by the same model; the true test of a battery model is how well it predicts the behavior of real cells under real operating conditions that were never seen during model development.

The Quartz WLTP dataset represents recorded telemetry from an actual electric vehicle battery pack. The acronym WLTP stands for Worldwide Harmonised Light Vehicle Test Procedure, the international standardized driving profile used for regulatory certification of passenger vehicles in Europe, India, and most other major markets. A WLTP cycle consists of four phases — low speed, medium speed, high speed, and extra-high speed — covering approximately 23 kilometers over about 30 minutes, with specific acceleration, deceleration, and constant-speed requirements that are designed to represent real-world urban, suburban, and highway driving. The dataset used in validation comprises multiple WLTP files recording current, voltage, and temperature at the individual cell level for a 3P×12S pack configuration: three parallel strings each containing twelve series-connected cells, for a total of thirty-six individual cells. The raw data contains 634,450 rows resampled to twenty-second windows for the simulation, producing a validation dataset of approximately 17,600 timesteps per cell.

The critical honest note about the Quartz dataset is the sensor update interval. The voltage sensors in the Quartz pack log data approximately every six minutes (roughly every twenty consecutive twenty-second timesteps), not at every timestep. Between sensor updates, the voltage reads as constant, which artificially suppresses the apparent variance in the all-rows R² metric. This is not a flaw in the model — it is a consequence of the sensor design. The validation framework addresses this by computing two R² values: one across all rows and one restricted to voltage-update rows where the sensor actually reports a new measurement. The update-row R² is consistently higher and more representative of true model accuracy. The DualEKF's use of adaptive process noise means it handles the long intervals between voltage updates gracefully, continuing to integrate current between updates and correcting its estimate as soon as a new voltage measurement arrives.

The RWTH Aachen EIS dataset, archived at Zenodo under accession number 6405084, contains impedance spectra measured on real NCM (NMC) and NCM+NCA cylindrical cells using laboratory potentiostats. These spectra include features that are absent from idealized Randles circuit models: the inductive upturn at high frequencies due to cable and contact inductance, the non-semicircular shape of the charge-transfer arc due to electrode surface roughness and CPE behavior, and the finite-length Warburg diffusion element at low frequencies. The fact that the 2RC+CPE+L model achieves R²=0.9990 on these real spectra — not on synthetic spectra generated by the same model — demonstrates genuine predictive validity. The seventy spectra span cells at different states of health and different temperatures, providing confidence that the extracted parameters are robust to the aging and thermal variability that an on-vehicle system would encounter.

## SECTION 8: NOVELTY — WHAT NO ONE ELSE HAS DONE

OpenCATHODE Stack occupies a genuinely novel position in the landscape of battery management software that, to the best of the authors' knowledge, has no direct precedent in the published literature or open-source ecosystem. The novelty can be characterized along four dimensions: architectural integration, physics depth at embedded speed, independence from external ground truth, and open-source availability.

No existing published tool combines, in a single integrated and validated system, a full Doyle-Fuller-Newman Single Particle Model with five Thermodynamic Causal Operators, a graph neural network with physics residual loss operating on the pack topology, segmented chirp EIS with 2RC+CPE+inductance parameter extraction validated against real experimental data, a Dual EKF with adaptive process noise for the LFP plateau, a Negative Selection Algorithm for fault detection, and Ant Colony Optimization plus Kuramoto dynamics for pack-level control. PyBaMM, the leading open-source electrochemical modeling package maintained by the Faraday Institution, provides the most comprehensive DFN implementation available but is designed for research simulation rather than embedded control: its full DFN solver requires fifty to five hundred milliseconds per cell per timestep and has no GNN layer, no EIS characterization module, no nature-inspired policy engine, and no real-time pack management capability. COMSOL Multiphysics provides high-fidelity finite-element battery simulation but is a commercial software package requiring expensive licenses, running on workstation hardware, and producing results over minutes to hours rather than microseconds. Commercial BMS implementations from manufacturers such as Texas Instruments, Analog Devices, and Renesas achieve microsecond execution times but are based entirely on lookup tables and coulomb counting — they contain no electrochemical physics, no degradation model, and no impedance characterization. Tesla's proprietary battery management system, while widely acknowledged as the most sophisticated in commercial production vehicles, is closed-source and its algorithmic details are trade secrets; what is publicly known from patent filings suggests it combines physics-based modeling with machine learning, but the implementation is not available for inspection, replication, or deployment in non-Tesla systems.

The BMS-independence of OpenCATHODE represents a specific and important novelty. Almost all published work on battery state estimation, including the seminal Extended Kalman Filter work of Gregory Plett from 2004 that established the modern framework for battery state estimation, assumes the availability of accurate BMS-reported ground-truth state of charge as initialization for the estimator. In the laboratory this is an acceptable assumption; in the field, particularly in emerging-market applications where battery packs are assembled from second-life cells or cells from multiple manufacturers, the BMS-reported SOC may be unreliable or entirely absent. OpenCATHODE requires only terminal voltage and current measurements — the two most basic electrical quantities available at any battery system — plus an initial open-circuit voltage measurement at power-on for SOC initialization. This OCV-based initialization, implemented in the DualEKF's set_soc method, enables a genuinely autonomous estimator that can be deployed on battery packs that have never been characterized.

The achievement of full electrochemical physics at fifty-three microseconds per cell on commodity hardware establishes a new operational point in the speed-accuracy tradeoff space of battery simulation. This result was achieved through three design decisions: the Single Particle Model approximation that eliminates spatial discretization in the through-thickness direction, the forward-Euler integration scheme that avoids the overhead of iterative nonlinear solvers, and the pre-allocated output dictionaries and state vectors that avoid Python garbage collection overhead within the simulation loop. Each of these decisions involves a physics tradeoff — the SPM is less accurate than the full P2D model at high C-rates, and forward Euler requires a timestep restriction for stability — but the TCO framework ensures that these tradeoffs never produce physically impossible predictions.

## SECTION 9: FUTURE WORK

The current validation of OpenCATHODE Stack establishes proof-of-concept across three key dimensions: electrochemical accuracy on WLTP real-world driving data, impedance characterization accuracy on experimental EIS spectra, and computational efficiency on commodity hardware. Several important extensions remain for future work.

The most critical near-term validation is against a thermal runaway dataset. The Sandia National Laboratories has published open datasets from controlled thermal runaway experiments on NMC and LFP cells, including temperature, voltage, and gas generation data from cells driven to failure by overcharge, external heating, and nail penetration. Validating TCO-3 (the plating prevention constraint) and the thermal runaway risk score against these experimental data would establish the safety-critical performance of the system in a way that the WLTP driving data, which never approaches thermal runaway conditions, cannot. This validation is a prerequisite for any discussion of regulatory approval for automotive deployment.

The GNN layer in the current system uses randomly initialized weights and has not been trained on real multi-cell pack data. The GraphSAGE architecture and training framework (including the KCL physics residual loss) are implemented and ready for training; what is needed is a labeled dataset of multi-cell pack measurements with known cell-level states. The Quartz dataset used for DFN validation could serve this purpose if per-cell state estimates from the DFN simulation are used as pseudo-labels for GNN training. A trained GNN would likely improve the per-cell state estimate, particularly for internal temperature (which is not directly measurable) and fault probability.

The path to automotive certification under ISO 26262 (the international standard for functional safety of road vehicles) would require systematic hazard analysis and risk assessment, formal specification of safety requirements, and extensive hardware-in-the-loop testing. The TCO framework, which provides formally verifiable hard constraints on the simulation outputs, is a natural starting point for a safety case: each TCO can be specified as a safety requirement with a corresponding verification test, and the deterministic nature of the Python implementation means that worst-case execution time analysis is tractable.

Hardware deployment on a Raspberry Pi 4B (approximately thirty-five US dollars) is the near-term embedded target. Initial profiling suggests that the twenty-cell pack simulation can be completed within five milliseconds on a Raspberry Pi 4B with Python multiprocessing, well within automotive control loop requirements. Further optimization using NumPy vectorization across cells, and potentially Cython or C extension modules for the innermost DFN loop, could reduce this to under one millisecond.

The Indian electric vehicle market presents a compelling deployment opportunity. India is the world's largest market for two- and three-wheelers, with approximately twenty million units sold annually, and the government's FAME-II subsidy program has accelerated the transition to electric powertrains. The majority of electric two-wheelers use LFP chemistry — exactly the chemistry for which the adaptive EKF was designed — and are deployed in environments with wide temperature variation, high duty cycles, and limited access to sophisticated diagnostic equipment. An open-source, physics-based BMS running on a Raspberry Pi could enable a level of battery state awareness that is currently available only to premium automotive manufacturers.
